Focus on the subjective.

The types of scenarios considered in many web performance studies/examples are really clean slate/pie in the sky scenarios that don't acknowledge the constraints within which we as devs operate. Sometimes you don't have a great deal of control over asset delivery, your application requires long asynchronous processes that cannot be easily improved, etc. We sell ourselves short by focusing on improving objective time when we can sometimes make much greater strides to how fast our apps & sites *feel* without improving their actual speed at all.

Be up front with your users.

If you know there's going to be a long wait, let the be clear and allow them to opt in. If you make it the user's choice, you give them agency and the wait feels shorter. Everyone's had an experience ordering a meal that's supposed to be quick, and when it takes forever it's *extra* annoying. When expecting something quick and it takes a long time, you're violating user's trust and defying their expectation. They haven't mentally prepared for the wait involved, and so they're left watching the proverbial tea kettle.
